01 Discovery

As one of the first steps I took a look at two competitors apps: Actionbound and c:geo. Especially Actionbound has a great educational concept which gave me valuable insights in how to trigger the user with rewards. By evaluating UX-related issues, I could define an appropriate standard.

User stories were defined to bring up a hypothesis for the audience and their usage of the app. The stories set the path as to where to look for my audience.

In a row of interviews, both remote and in-person, with potential travellers using the app, I gathered valuable insights into their needs, goals and painpoints when traveling and beeing out and about. All answers were recorded and classified using affinity mapping. The classified answers lead me to three user personas (two main personas, one secondary persona) which served as base for further developing.

To have a look at the entire interview preparation including a survey (set up on Google Forms) visit this document.

Affinity maps helped define the needs, goals and pain points of my interviewees.

Understanding the problem

Motivation

  • Creating good memories with taking tours fitting the users personality
  • Compact tours with a good amount of information
  • Experiencing something special

Frustration

  • Tours matching personal needs/mood are hard to find
  • Basic information regarding length, scope and level of tours is too scattered
  • Available tours are often too generic
  • Reviews are much needed to determine if tours are worth taking

Key insights

Filter options

Acknowledge the users needs: set up appropriate filters to narrow down search results (more filter options are still in-progress)

Quick overview

Establishing a card-like system with a picture and the most important parameters to easily grasp the content of the tour. For adding personality and as this an app used for traveling, the cards are presented as Polaroid-like pictures, like in a photo album with only the best shots represented.

Reviews

To help others find what they need, install a review option. Within the review screen, let the user select the circumstances in which the tour was taken in (single, group, family, etc)

Categories

Set up main categories (outdoor, history, art, family, nightlife) to help users quickly decide where to look. These categories can be refined in a later step.

Offline use

Let the user create a board where they can save favourite tours for later use. This way, exploring can be prepared at home and tours can also be viewed offline.

Specialisation

To let users pick an exciting and special tour, install a payment screen to give access to paid tour providers.

02 Concepting

My full fledged user personas were then taken as model to derive process flows in which their key actions found place.

Using a co-existing hierarchy, I developed the first sitemap which contained all crucial actions I found in the flows. To continously determine if I my focus was still on the right path, I condudcted an online open card sort with ten participants. According to the findings from the card sort, I had to revise and refine my site map.

Armed with all the valuable insights, findings and deliverables I could gather so far, I used rapid prototyping to process these into digital mid-fidelity wireframes. By constantly refining these wireframes, they were then translated into high-fidelity wireframes and finally, I had my very first digital prototype which was ready to test with my users.

03 Prototyping & Testing

The prototype was ready to be tested!

I conducted moderated user tests with five participants to determine the overall usability and design. The testing went overall good and no catatrophes were discovered. The ranking of issues took place according to Jakob Nielsens scale of errors. All results from testing were gathered and classified in an affinity map and a rainbow spreadsheet.

Armed with the new insights from testing, I took on another round of iterating the prototype. To accomplish a prototype which not only shows the revised screens but also usability standards, I included a range of Gestalt Principles the design principles defined by Google (functionality and visual clarity )and WCAG guidelines (accessability) found their way into my work as well. The revised prototype went into testing with fellow designers from Slack. For making it easier to absolve future iterations and developing the app, I installed a Design Language System.
